How it Works

The basics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) is that there are actually two deep learning models. One that generates images (the generator) and one that tries to tell if the image is real or fake (the discriminator). The generator will continually make images while the discriminator tries to tell if the images are real or fake. The first couple iterations will be obvious fakes and the discriminator will easily flag them as fake. However, the generator will continue to make tweaks to the image with each set being slightly more believable. This continues until, ideally, the discriminator is no longer able to tell the difference between the two. There are certain ruts that this process can fall into such as mode collapse but this short summary won't cover them.

I actually tried two different methods to achieve the results that I did. I used a Wasserstein GAN as well as a Style GAN. These are two different ways to achieve the same generative results. My understanding of the difference is that Style GAN does an excellent job separating "high-level attributes" as NVIDIA puts it. This means things like parts of a face such as freckles, hair, etc. In theory I thought that this would do a good job isolating arms, legs, features, etc. that Pokemon have. However, when do you know when you have a good looking Pokemon? There's a lot of subjectivity in what looks believable. That's where Wasserstein GANs come in. The generator starts out with random noise and makes slight tweaks to the image. As the generator learns more it replaces noise with features. There's a lot more objectivity here as the amount of noise is usually correlated to a better result. In theory and according to papers it was a marked improvement in the world of GANs. However, I ended up using my Style GAN result. This will be talked about in lessons learned.
